# Content types

Each content type has different structural requirements, keyword density targets, and
quality expectations. Read the section for your content type before outlining.

## Table of contents

1. Blog post (800–1400 words)
2. Short description (80–200 words)
3. Category description (200–500 words)
4. Landing page (500–1000 words)

---

## 1. Blog post

**Word count:** 800–1400 words (can go longer if the topic demands it — don't pad to hit
a number, and don't cut substance to stay under a cap)

**Purpose:** Rank for informational or commercial-investigation keywords. Build topical
authority. Drive organic traffic to the site.

**Structure:**

```
SEO Title (50-60 chars)
Meta Description (150-155 chars)

H1: [Main heading — can be a question or a statement]

Intro (2-3 paragraphs):
- Hook the reader in the first sentence
- State what they'll learn or get from this article
- Primary keyword in the first paragraph

H2: [Topic section 1]
  Body paragraphs (2-4 sentences each)
  H3: [Subsection if section covers multiple subtopics]

H2: [Topic section 2]
  ...

H2: [Topic section 3-5 depending on depth needed]
  ...

H2: FAQ (3-5 questions)
  Q: [Question targeting a long-tail or PAA query]
  A: [40-60 word answer — concise enough for a featured snippet]

Conclusion (1-2 paragraphs):
- Summarize the key takeaway (not a restatement of the intro)
- Clear CTA: what should the reader do next?
```

**Keyword density:** Primary keyword 3-5 times total across the whole piece (including
title, H1, meta). That works out to roughly once every 300 words plus the metadata slots.
Don't count — just write naturally and check at the end.

**What makes a blog post rank:**
- It answers the search query better than the current top 3 results
- It has a clear angle or point of view (not a Wikipedia summary)
- It includes something the competitors don't: original data, a practical framework,
  real examples, or a contrarian take backed by evidence
- It's scannable — someone in a hurry can get the answer from headings and bold text
- FAQ section targets questions the competitors skip

**Common mistakes:**
- Opening with "In today's digital landscape..." or any variant of "In this article,
  we'll explore..."
- Every section being exactly the same length (looks algorithmic)
- Bullet lists with one-line items that should be prose
- Hedging everything ("might," "could potentially," "may help")
- Generic conclusion ("By following these tips, you'll...")

## 2. Short description

**Word count:** 80–200 words

**Purpose:** Product descriptions, service summaries, or brief blurbs that appear on
listing pages, Google Shopping, or as excerpts. These need to pack maximum information
into minimum space.

**Structure:**

```
[Opening sentence: what it is + primary keyword + key differentiator]

[2-3 sentences: most important features/benefits, not a feature dump]

[Closing sentence: who it's for or what problem it solves]
```

No headings. No bullet lists unless displaying specs. No FAQ.

**Keyword density:** Primary keyword once in the first sentence and once more somewhere
in the body. Two occurrences in 80-200 words is enough.

**What makes a short description effective:**
- Specificity. "Waterproof Bluetooth speaker with 24-hour battery" beats "High-quality
  portable speaker with great features."
- Benefit-first language. Lead with what the reader gets, not what the product has.
- No filler. Every word earns its place or gets cut.

**Common mistakes:**
- Starting with the brand name instead of the product/benefit
- Using promotional adjectives without substance ("amazing," "best-in-class,"
  "revolutionary")
- Trying to cram too many keywords into too little space (reads like spam)
- Duplicate or near-duplicate descriptions across similar products

## 3. Category description

**Word count:** 200–500 words

**Purpose:** Sit at the top of category/collection pages on e-commerce or content sites.
These pages often rank for high-volume head terms. The description helps Google understand
the page's topic while giving users useful context.

**Structure:**

```
[Opening paragraph: 2-3 sentences defining the category + primary keyword.
 Answer the implicit question: "What will I find on this page?"]

[Middle section: 1-2 paragraphs covering:
 - What the category includes (product types, subcategories, topic range)
 - Key buying criteria or selection guidance
 - 2-3 semantic keywords worked in naturally]

[Optional: Brief FAQ or buying guide snippet — 1-2 Q&As max]

[Closing: 1-2 sentences with a soft CTA — browse, filter, compare]
```

**Keyword density:** Primary keyword 2-3 times. One semantic keyword per paragraph
is plenty.

**Category descriptions are tricky because:**
- They need to be useful without getting in the way of the product grid or content listing
- Google wants content on category pages, but users want products/content — not essays
- Too short and you lose ranking signals. Too long and users bounce past it.
- They're often templated across dozens of categories. Resist the urge to use the same
  structure with swapped nouns.

**What makes a category description rank:**
- It contextualizes the category with buying guidance or selection criteria
- It mentions subcategories or product types (internal linking opportunities)
- It answers "what's the difference between X and Y" if the category has confusing
  overlaps
- It's scannable — the user who doesn't need it can skip it without friction

**Common mistakes:**
- Writing a full article when 300 words would do
- Generic category-swap content ("Browse our wide selection of [category]")
- Missing internal links to subcategories or related categories
- Keyword-stuffing the first sentence ("Looking for [keyword]? Our [keyword] collection
  has the best [keyword] for every [keyword] need.")

## 4. Landing page

**Word count:** 500–1000 words

**Purpose:** Rank for commercial or transactional keywords. Convert visitors into leads
or customers. These pages serve dual masters: SEO and conversion.

**Structure:**

```
SEO Title (50-60 chars, keyword + value prop)
Meta Description (150-155 chars, keyword + CTA hint)

H1: [Clear value proposition with primary keyword]

Hero section (1-2 sentences):
- What you offer + who it's for
- Primary keyword in the first sentence

H2: [Problem/pain point section]
  2-3 paragraphs on the problem you solve

H2: [Solution/how it works]
  Your approach — be specific about what makes it different
  Can use a numbered list (3-5 steps) if there's a clear process

H2: [Social proof / results]
  Specific numbers, named clients, case study snippets
  NOT "trusted by thousands" — say "used by 3,200 teams"

H2: [FAQ — 2-3 questions]
  Target objections disguised as questions
  "How long does setup take?" "What if I need to cancel?"

CTA section:
  Clear, single action the reader should take
```

**Keyword density:** Primary keyword 2-4 times. Landing pages are shorter, so err on the
lower end. The conversion copy matters more than keyword saturation.

**What makes a landing page rank AND convert:**
- The H1 makes the value proposition immediately clear
- Every section moves the reader closer to the CTA — no tangents
- Social proof is specific (numbers, names, results) not vague ("our clients love us")
- Objections are handled in the FAQ, not ignored
- The page loads fast and works perfectly on mobile

**Common mistakes:**
- Writing it like a blog post (too much educational content, not enough sell)
- Writing it like a sales page (too much sell, not enough substance for Google)
- Multiple competing CTAs ("Sign up! Also, read our blog! Also, follow us!")
- Missing FAQ section (landing pages that rank almost always have FAQs)
- Hero section that's all benefits and no specifics
