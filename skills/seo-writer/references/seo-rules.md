# SEO rules

These are the on-page SEO rules applied during outlining and drafting. They're based on
what actually correlates with ranking, not SEO folklore.

## 1. Keyword placement

Where the primary keyword must appear:
- SEO title (within the first 5 words when possible)
- Meta description (anywhere — but earlier is better)
- H1 heading
- First paragraph (first 100 words of body content)
- At least one H2 heading (naturally — don't force it if it reads badly)
- Conclusion or final section

Where secondary/semantic keywords should appear:
- Scattered across H2/H3 headings
- Within body paragraphs where they fit the flow
- In FAQ questions and answers
- In image alt text (if images are included)

**Keyword density is not a ranking factor.** Google has said this repeatedly. What matters
is that the keyword appears in the right structural positions (title, H1, first paragraph)
and that the content thoroughly covers the topic. If you find yourself counting keyword
instances, you're doing it wrong.

**Keyword stuffing signals to avoid:**
- Same exact-match keyword in consecutive sentences
- Keyword in every single heading
- Keyword appearing more than once per 200 words (rough guideline, not a rule)
- Awkward phrasing to preserve exact-match ("best cheap running shoes for flat feet
  beginners" crammed into a sentence as-is)

**Natural keyword variants are fine and encouraged:**
- Plural/singular forms
- Reordered words ("running shoes for beginners" / "beginner running shoes")
- Related terms that a human would naturally use when discussing the topic

## 2. Title tag optimization

**Format:** [Primary keyword phrase] — [Value prop or modifier] | [Brand]

**Character limit:** 50-60 characters (Google truncates after ~60)

**Rules:**
- Primary keyword as close to the start as possible
- Don't sacrifice readability for keyword position — if the keyword sounds awkward at the
  start, move it slightly back
- Include a modifier that adds value: year, "Guide," "How to," a number, or a benefit
- Brand name goes at the end after a pipe or dash (or omit if space is tight)
- Never use ALL CAPS
- Avoid clickbait that the content doesn't deliver on

**Good examples:**
- "Running Shoes for Flat Feet: 8 Options Tested in 2025"
- "How to Fix a Leaking Faucet Without Calling a Plumber"
- "CRM Software for Small Business — Features, Pricing, Reviews"

**Bad examples:**
- "Best Running Shoes | Great Running Shoes | Buy Running Shoes Online"
- "AMAZING Guide to Everything You Need to Know About Running Shoes!!!"
- "Running Shoes" (wastes 40 characters of space)

## 3. Meta description optimization

**Character limit:** 150-155 characters (Google truncates after ~155)

**Purpose:** Not a direct ranking factor, but affects click-through rate (CTR), which
indirectly affects rankings. The meta description is your ad copy in the SERP.

**Rules:**
- Include the primary keyword (Google bolds matching terms in search results)
- Start with the most compelling part — what the reader gets
- End with an implicit or explicit CTA
- Match the search intent: informational queries want "Learn how..." while commercial
  queries want "Compare features..."
- Don't use quotes (Google truncates at quotation marks)
- Don't duplicate meta descriptions across pages

**Good examples:**
- "Tested 8 running shoes for flat feet across 500+ miles. Here's which ones actually
  reduce pain — and which ones are overhyped."
- "Compare CRM software features and pricing for small businesses. Free trials, honest
  reviews, no affiliate bias."

**Bad examples:**
- "Welcome to our website. We offer the best running shoes for flat feet. Click here to
  learn more about our products."
- "[Keyword]. [Keyword] guide. Best [keyword] 2025. Read our [keyword] article."

## 4. Heading hierarchy

**H1:** One per page. Contains the primary keyword. This is the article's main heading
(which may differ slightly from the SEO title tag).

**H2:** Main section headings. Each H2 should:
- Be descriptive enough that a scanning reader understands the section's content
- Work as a question or clear topic (not "Section 1" or "More Info")
- Include a semantic keyword where it fits naturally

**H3:** Subsections within an H2. Use when a section has distinct subtopics that deserve
their own heading. Don't use H3 just to make the article look more structured.

**Heading rules:**
- Never skip levels (H1 → H3 without an H2 between them)
- Don't use headings for styling (making text bigger)
- Every heading should tell the reader what they'll learn in that section
- Avoid heading questions that can be answered with "yes" or "no" — they produce
  thin sections
- Keep headings under 70 characters

## 5. Featured snippet targeting

Google pulls featured snippets from pages that answer questions concisely and clearly.
To optimize for them:

**Paragraph snippets (most common):**
- Answer the question in 40-60 words directly after the H2 that poses or implies the
  question
- Use the question's language in the answer
- Then expand with details below

**List snippets:**
- Use a clear H2 ("How to..." or "Steps to..." or "Types of...")
- Follow with an ordered or unordered list
- Keep list items to one line each with 4-8 items total

**Table snippets:**
- Use HTML tables or markdown tables for comparison data
- Include clear column headers
- Keep to 3-5 columns and 4-8 rows

**FAQ snippets:**
- Each Q should be in a heading tag (H3 or even just bold + paragraph)
- Answer immediately — don't lead with context
- Keep each answer to 40-60 words
- Target questions from Google's "People Also Ask" box
- Prefer less common questions that competitors skip — the obvious ones are harder to
  win and less valuable when you do

## 6. Internal linking

**Why it matters:** Internal links distribute page authority, help Google discover and
understand page relationships, and keep users on your site.

**Rules:**
- Link to 2-5 relevant internal pages per 1000 words
- Use descriptive anchor text (not "click here" or "read more")
- Link early in the content when possible (links in the first few paragraphs carry
  slightly more weight)
- Link to both pillar/cornerstone content and related posts
- Don't link to the same page twice in one article
- Don't over-link — if every other sentence has a link, it's distracting

**Anchor text guidelines:**
- Use natural phrases that describe the target page
- Vary anchor text — don't always use the exact same phrase
- It's fine for anchor text to include a keyword, but don't force exact-match keyword
  anchors on every internal link

## 7. URL structure

**Format:** /primary-keyword or /category/primary-keyword

**Rules:**
- Lowercase only
- Hyphens between words (not underscores)
- No stop words unless they aid readability ("how-to-fix-a-leaky-faucet" not
  "how-fix-leaky-faucet")
- Short but descriptive: 3-5 words is ideal
- Include the primary keyword
- Don't change URLs after publishing unless you set up proper 301 redirects

## 8. Content freshness signals

Some queries are "freshness-sensitive" — Google prioritizes recent content for them.
Signals that indicate freshness:

- Date in the title or meta description (use the current year for annual/updated guides)
- "Updated [month] [year]" near the top of the article
- Recent data, statistics, or references within the content
- Updated publication date in structured data

**When to include freshness signals:**
- "Best [X] 2025" type queries — always
- Fast-changing topics (tech, regulations, pricing) — usually
- Evergreen topics (basic how-tos, definitions) — rarely needed

**Don't fake freshness.** Changing the date without updating the content is a quick way
to lose trust with both readers and Google.
